Credit Lessons

Use this as the educational part of your app.

Credit utilization is the balance game.

Utilization is how much of your credit limit you are using. For example, if your card has a $500 limit and a $250 balance, that card is at 50% utilization.

%
Why it mattersHigh utilization can make your profile look risky, even if you pay on time.
Simple targetMany people aim under 30%, then under 10% when they want a cleaner profile.
Example: A $500 limit with a $238 balance is 47.6% utilization. To report under 10%, the balance would need to be around $50 or less.

The app should not promise that lowering utilization will increase a score by a specific amount. A safer message is: “Lower utilization may help your credit profile look cleaner, but results vary.”

Statement date is not the same as due date.

The due date is when payment is due. The statement closing date is when the billing cycle ends, and that balance may be what gets reported.

⏰
Due datePay by this date to avoid late fees and protect payment history.
Statement datePay before this date if you want a lower balance to report.
Example: If your due date is May 20 but your statement closes May 25, paying before May 25 can help the lower balance show up on the next statement.

This is one of the best features for your app because many beginners only watch the due date and forget about what balance gets reported.

Secured cards can be a rebuilding tool.

A secured card usually requires a deposit. That deposit often becomes the credit limit. It can help users build payment history when used carefully.

🔒
Good forNew credit, rebuilding credit, or users who keep getting denied for unsecured cards.
Watch outFees, low limits, and cards that do not clearly report to major credit bureaus.
Example: A $200 secured card can still help if the user keeps the reported balance low and pays on time.

Do not describe secured cards as guaranteed approval. Say they may be a more realistic category for some rebuilding users.

Applying too fast can work against you.

When someone applies for credit, it can create a hard inquiry. One inquiry is usually not the end of the world, but many recent inquiries can make a profile look risky.

⚠️
Smart moveCompare categories first and avoid applying randomly.
App featureWarn users when they say they have 3+ recent inquiries.
Example: If a user has five recent inquiries and high utilization, the app should suggest slowing down and lowering balances first.

This helps your app feel like a coach instead of just a card ad machine.

Never promise approval.

Your app can show categories that may fit a profile, but the lender makes the real decision. That decision can depend on full credit history, income, debt, identity checks, and lender rules.

⚖️
Bad wording“You are approved,” “guaranteed approval,” or “this will raise your score.”
Safer wording“This category may fit your profile. Approval is not guaranteed.”
Affiliate rule: If you earn money from card links later, disclose that clearly near the card recommendation and Apply button.

This lesson protects the product because financial apps can get in trouble when they make recommendations look more certain or unbiased than they really are.

Glossary

Simple explanations for users who are new to credit.

Credit limit

The most the card issuer allows you to borrow on that card.

Balance

How much you currently owe on the card.

Utilization

Your balance divided by your credit limit. A $100 balance on a $500 limit is 20% utilization.

Statement closing date

The date your billing cycle ends. The balance around this date may be reported.

Due date

The date your payment is due. Missing this can cause fees and hurt payment history.

Hard inquiry

A credit check that can happen when you apply for credit.

Secured card

A card that usually requires a refundable deposit and may be useful for rebuilding.

Annual fee

A yearly fee charged by some cards. Beginners should compare this carefully.
